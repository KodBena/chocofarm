# Independent consult verdict — chocofarm adaptive-stochastic-orienteering

## (1) One-paragraph verdict

The author's *qualitative* contention — that adaptivity should pay in this problem — is plausible and the proof-by-construction is sound in principle. But the diagnosis of *why the numbers don't show it* is **incomplete and partly mis-attributed**. The two named artifacts (unit values; max-of-sample-means bias) are both real, but they are not the dominant cause. The dominant cause is a **modeling/implementation flaw in the detector representation**: the `cover_mask` built from each region's single `representative_point()` collapses most of the disjunctive structure the whole model is built to exercise — roughly 11 of the 17 area-overlap pairs vanish and 8 of 16 detectors degenerate to singleton "observe-this-one-treasure" probes. On top of that, the rollout's value comes from a **detector-blind greedy leaf** whose only lever under unit values is travel-order, so information has almost nothing to act on; and the headline comparison mixes inconsistent λ operating points at an 80-run noise floor that is far larger than the ±1.5% deltas being reported. So: adaptivity may well pay, but the current harness could not detect it even if it did, and "unit values" is being asked to carry an explanation that the geometry bug and the leaf policy explain better.

## (2) Per-claim critique

**Claim 1 — "Adaptivity genuinely pays" (proof by construction).**
Accepted as a structural argument, with a caveat. The human-contingent-policy story (bank an early cluster, beeline to the near exit) is a real source of value: the *early-exit option* is genuinely adaptive and no fixed prefix can encode "exit early *iff* the first cluster paid out." This is the strongest part of the case and it does not depend on heterogeneous values — it is a *variance/timing* gain on the renewal cycle (you stop paying travel once a good-enough basket is banked). The caveat: the magnitude of that gain is bounded by how non-monotone the realized reward-per-time is, and under exactly-5-of-20 with unit values the per-run reward is tightly concentrated (you will collect close to 0.25 × visited regardless), so the early-exit option is worth *something* but plausibly little. The construction proves existence, not magnitude — and the author treats it as if it proves magnitude.

**Claim 2 — "two artifacts: (a) unit values, (b) max-bias readout."**
- (a) is **directionally right but for the wrong reason.** The author says unit values mute the margin because "you collect all present anyway and there is nothing of differing worth to chase." The deeper reason is that under unit values, *detector information cannot change the collect/skip decision* — every present treasure is worth exactly 1, so sharpening a marginal from 0.25 only reorders/skips travel; it never changes *what is worth collecting*. So the information's only cash value is avoided travel to absent treasures, a second-order effect. That is a real muting, but it is entangled with the leaf-policy flaw below, so "unit values" alone is not a clean explanation.
- (b) is **correct and well-reasoned.** `sparse_value` (chocobo_baseline_sparse.py:38–55) takes `max` over actions of sample-means, which is upward-biased (Jensen / winner's-curse), and the author's own evidence is decisive: d1/C8 → +0.21 but d1/C32 → −0.03, i.e. *more samples drove the estimate toward zero/negative*. That monotone-toward-the-floor behaviour is the signature of maximization bias, not signal. The conclusion "the least-biased point reads ≈ tie" is the right read of that table. Using the *induced policy's measured rate* instead of the root value is the correct fix for this specific bias.

**Claim 3 — "unbiased policy-rate MC + heterogeneous values; sparse sampling stays the anchor."**
Half-right. Unbiased policy-rate MC is necessary and cheap — endorse. Heterogeneous values is a reasonable regime to *also* test, but it is being positioned as *the* fix, and it is premature: you should not introduce heterogeneous values until the detector geometry is fixed and the MC noise floor is brought below the effect size, or you will simply re-run a harness that cannot resolve the answer. "Sparse sampling stays the convergent anchor" is fine in theory but currently the anchor inherits the *same broken cover_mask* (it imports `M.cover_mask`, baseline_sparse.py:17,24,35) and the same detector-blind `simulate_base` leaf (baseline_sparse.py:48), so the anchor is anchored to the wrong model.

**Claim 4 — "τ_4 teleport likely dominated."**
Plausible and probably true, but the *evidence offered is too weak to assert it.* "8/80 reads as its own noise" is honest, and the geometry supports the conclusion (τ_4 at (4.15,13.74) is a far NE corner; d(CSNE,τ_4)=12.28, d(CSCE,τ_4)=13.25 — it is the most distant teleport and adjacent only to the four NE treasures). But greedy "never used it across 2,500 runs" is partly an artifact: greedy's exit is `nearest_exit` (line 75) computed *geometrically*, and τ_4 is rarely the nearest exit by construction, so non-use is nearly tautological rather than an economic verdict. The claim is likely correct; the argument for it is circular. A one-line dominance check (below) would settle it.

## (3) Ranked list of the most likely real flaws

**1. Detector cover-mask collapses the disjunctive structure (HIGHEST).**
`chocobo_stage2_approx.py:50–55` builds `cover_mask[i]` from a *single* `representative_point()` of region `i` and the set of regions containing *that one point*. The geometry script (chocobo_geometry.py:109–116) found overlaps by *area intersection* and recorded **17 overlapping pairs** in `chocobo_instance.json["overlaps"]`. A stdlib point-in-polygon reconstruction (approximate on the rep-point, but the mechanism is exact) shows the rep-point method recovers only ~6 of those pairs and leaves **8 of 16 detectors as singletons** (D_0, D_1, D_2, D_5, D_6, D_7, D_17, D_18 see only themselves); pairs like (0,1),(0,2),(1,15),(5,7),(6,7),(17,18) and most of the 8/9/10/11/12 cluster's cross-links are dropped. Evidence: the two files compute *different* overlap notions and the parsed `overlaps` array (17 pairs) is never used by the solver. Consequence: the model the author is testing is mostly a set of *exact single-treasure probes*, not the disjunctive sensor the contention is about. This is the single likeliest reason "adaptive information has nothing to do," and it is a modeling flaw, not a unit-value artifact. **Caveat on my evidence:** I could not run shapely (not installed in any reachable env), so the exact rep-points differ from mine; the *direction and magnitude* of the collapse should be confirmed with the real `representative_point()` — see experiment E1.

**2. The rollout/sparse leaf is detector-blind greedy (HIGH).**
`simulate_base` (lines 111–132) ranks only on `marg[i]*value - lam*d`. After a detector splits the belief, the base sees sharpened marginals but, under unit values, the only thing sharper marginals can do is change travel order / skip near-zero treasures. So even where a detector *does* carry disjunctive information (the surviving ~6 pairs), the value of that information is throttled by the leaf. Rolling out over a base that cannot *act* on information understates adaptive value structurally. This compounds flaw 1.

**3. Noise floor swamps the reported deltas (HIGH).**
Rollout rate is measured at **80 runs** (line 247; note `ROLL_RUNS=40` at line 35 is dead — never referenced), greedy at 2500 (line 237). A per-run reward of R∈{0..5} and a ratio estimator at 80 runs has a standard error on the order of several percent — larger than the +1.5% rollout-vs-greedy and the −3.9%-ish rollout-vs-static being reported. The headline comparison cannot resolve effects of the size claimed. The author's own "8/80 is noise" remark applies to the *entire* rollout row, not just τ_4.

**4. Inconsistent λ operating points in the comparison (MEDIUM).**
`static_rate()` (209–222) uses no λ (pure ER/T best prefix). Greedy is measured at `lam_g`, rollout at a *separately* re-estimated `lam_r` (242–245, only 3 iterations at 30 runs each). Policy *improvement* is a one-step argmax at a *fixed* λ; comparing greedy-at-λ_g against rollout-at-λ_r conflates "better policy" with "different operating point." At a true Dinkelbach fixed point rate≈λ so each number is a self-consistent *rate* (so direct comparison is defensible as rates), but the rollout's λ_r is a 30-run-noisy fixed point, so its rate readout sits at a mis-estimated operating point. Decouple by measuring all three policies' *rates* at a single common λ (or just report each policy's own unbiased rate at large N and compare those).

**5. Static baseline is simultaneously a strawman *and* unfair-to-beat (MEDIUM, cuts both ways).**
`static_rate()` is the *best prefix of one NN route* — not the best static route (Stage-1's `static_optimal_rate`, chocobo_stage1.py:230–249, does the real combinatorial optimum). So it is a *weak* static opponent in principle. But the prefix-rate curve I computed is bimodal (peak L=9 at 0.0826, dip L=11 at 0.0692, higher peak L=16 at 0.0855), and "best prefix" cherry-picks the global max truncation — which is a *non-causal* oracle (you cannot know at runtime that L=16 is the best truncation of a route whose realized rewards you haven't seen). So as a *static* policy it is slightly optimistic (oracle truncation) yet as an *opponent* it is weak (NN, not optimal static). The net is that the static number 0.0855 is not a clean reference line; it should be replaced by Stage-1's true static optimum on a tractable sub-instance, or by an honestly-realizable fixed route.

**6. Renewal-cycle / fixed-entry assumption (LOW–MEDIUM).**
Single fixed entry CSNE, best geometric exit, TELE_OH=12 in map units where the treasure bbox diagonal is ~17.9 — so the teleport overhead is ~0.7× a full-map traverse, *cheap*. That cheapness is exactly what makes "sweep 16 of 20 then exit" optimal and mutes early-exit value. This is an *uncalibrated-units* risk the author already flags (the whole rate is treasures/map-unit), but it directly governs whether adaptivity pays: if real teleport+travel times make a full sweep expensive, early-exit adaptivity gets much more valuable. The conclusion "adaptive ≈ static" is conditional on TELE_OH=12 and symmetric Euclidean travel, and that conditionality is under-stated in the contention.

## (4) Endorse-or-redirect on the next step

**Redirect, with a reordering.** The proposed step (unbiased policy-rate MC + heterogeneous values) is partially right but skips the load-bearing fix. Concrete ordering:

1. **Fix the detector model first (blocking).** Replace the single-representative-point `cover_mask` with the *true* disjunctive cover. Two clean options: (a) for each detector, define its observation as "≥1 present among the treasures whose regions cover the *point the agent actually stands at*," and choose that point as a real interior point of region `i` *and* explicitly union in every region whose polygon intersects `i` (use the already-computed `overlaps` array in the instance JSON — it is sitting unused); or (b) drop the point abstraction and let a detector action mean "enter region `i`," with the cover set = `{j : region_j ∩ region_i ≠ ∅ at the entry point}`. Either way the 17 area-overlaps must be representable. This is the difference between testing the model and testing a degenerate shadow of it.

2. **Make the leaf information-aware (blocking for rollout fidelity).** The greedy leaf should at least be allowed to *skip determined-absent* treasures and *prioritize determined-present* ones — under unit values that is the only channel by which sharpened beliefs change behaviour, and it must be open or the rollout is structurally blind.

3. **Then unbiased policy-rate MC** at a *single common λ* with N large enough (≥5,000, ideally 20,000 as Stage-1 already uses) that the standard error is < 1%. Report greedy, rollout, sparse-induced policy, and a *realizable* static route, all at the same λ and N. This is the honest metric the author already identified for the max-bias problem.

4. **Only then heterogeneous values**, and treat it as a *diagnostic sweep* (value-spread parameter from unit → high-variance) so you can see the adaptive margin grow with value heterogeneity rather than asserting it. Real redis values later is fine; synthetic-with-a-knob first.

On the **alternative anchors** the prompt raises: a **cluster decomposition** (exact within geographic neighbourhoods, chained as a max-ratio cycle) is the most attractive *near-term* addition — the geometry is strongly clustered (the 8/9/10/11/12 NW pack; the 13/14/15 pack; the 0/1/2/17/18 SE pack), and exactly-5-of-20 makes per-cluster exact belief-MDP tractable (each cluster sees few treasures, so its world count is small). That would give a *trustworthy* upper-ish reference that the sparse sampler currently cannot, and it directly attacks "is exact-within-neighbourhood adaptivity worth more than static." A full POMDP solver is overkill and infeasible (the author already settled exact is infeasible). So: keep sparse sampling as the *convergent* anchor, but add **cluster-exact** as the *trusted* anchor — it is cheaper to trust than to push sparse-sampling width up.

## (5) What's missing, and the cheapest experiment that would tell us adaptivity does *not* pay

**Missing:** an experiment designed to *detect a null result*, not assume one. Everything in the harness is set up to show adaptivity winning; nothing is set up to *bound* its maximum possible value. The contention never states "here is the most adaptivity could be worth, and we measured it at X."

**Cheapest decisive experiments (in order, all bounded — no 15,504-world parallel solver):**

- **E0 — confirm the geometry bug (minutes, read-only).** Run the rep-point cover construction with the *real* shapely `representative_point()` and print, per detector, the cover set, then diff against the 17-pair `overlaps` array. If the collapse I found reproduces, that alone reframes the whole status doc. (I could not run shapely here; this is the one thing to verify before anything else.)

- **E1 — value-of-information upper bound (the null-detector).** Compute two rates with the *same* base policy: (i) ordinary online belief; (ii) a **clairvoyant** policy that is told the true world for free at entry (no detector cost, perfect info). The gap `rate_clairvoyant − rate_static` is the *absolute ceiling* on what any adaptive sensing could buy. If that ceiling is itself small (say < a few %) under unit values, then **adaptivity provably cannot pay much regardless of detector fidelity**, and the author's claim-1 magnitude is refuted for this instance — heterogeneous values become the *only* hope. This is one MC pass with a trivial policy change (`marg[i]` → known presence), cheaper than the rollout already being run. This is the single experiment that most directly answers "could adaptivity genuinely not pay, and how would we detect it."

- **E2 — early-exit isolation.** Run the static best-prefix route but with an *adaptive stopping rule* (exit as soon as banked reward × something exceeds the marginal continuation rate at current λ), everything else identical. The gap between adaptive-stop and fixed-prefix isolates the early-exit value with no detector dependence — it tests the strongest part of claim 1 directly and cheaply.

- **E3 — τ_4 dominance (one line).** For each teleport `k`, compute `min over treasures present-set of` whether `k` is ever the cheapest exit *given the realized basket*; or more simply, report the count of runs in which τ_4 is the geometric nearest exit (it is rarely, by the coordinates). Replaces the circular "greedy never chose it" with an economic statement.

If E0 reproduces the cover collapse and E1's clairvoyant ceiling is small, the honest revised status is: *"adaptive sensing cannot pay much on this instance under unit values for a structural reason (low VoI), independent of any readout bias; the path forward is heterogeneous values and/or a recalibrated time model, and the prior conclusion was confounded by a detector-representation bug."* If E1's ceiling is large but measured adaptive gain is near zero, the detector bug (flaw 1) and the blind leaf (flaw 2) are the culprits, not unit values.
